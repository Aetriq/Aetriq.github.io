Chimera iXY — Rev. 1.0

Official documentation for the Chimera iXY — a DIY CoreXY build combining parts from two broken 3D printers.

1.0 Introduction

This document covers the detailed documentation of my DIY CoreXY build from two broken 3D printers, named ‘Chimera iXY’. The primary objective of this document is the keep track of everything as the printer was being built; this includes design choices, assemblies, etc. as well as to demonstrate that Reprap printers can still be built with modern printers that contain OEM and proprietary parts.

It is important to note that this document is semi-formal, as it doesn’t follow any standard documentation formatting; I frequently update various sections and subsections as I went along. The majority of this document isn’t graphically focused, e.g. it doesn’t fully contain diagrams for each section that requires one; instead, an entire (summarized) diagram will be provided for each major section.

2.0 Background

This project started when received a broken Mingda Magician X (Version 1, not 2). The mainboard and X-carriage daughterboard has shorted out due to a cheaply designed 20-pin ribbon cable; after further evaluation I found out that this was due to a poorly crimped cable that shorted out, most likely between two high current wires since it has burn marks. The only wires that drew enough current were the heater cartridge wire; after inspecting the traces, MOSFETs and fuses on the mainboard, they all were intact, thus the issue is likely to be caused by one of the TMC2208s constantly shorting out, preventing the board from booting at all. Alot of additional parts seemed to have been damaged by this short, including the filament runout sensor, strain gauge sensor (for auto bed leveling) and some other insignificant sensors that I will most likely not be using since they are shape and wiring are very proprietary. This printer remained as the ‘modern half’ of this build.

The second 3D printer used for this was the Flsun i3 2017, or Flsun i3 C. This was an older 3D printer I’ve been using for quite some time, and when it did it’s job, it did it pretty well. A lot of it was stock, it uses a MKS Gen L v1.0 which supports TMC2208s only in standalone mode. However, it was a very finicky machine, it would frequently run into ‘ghost’ thermal runaway errors even after tuning the firmware. There are also some other modern features I wanted that unfortunately this printer couldn’t take advantage of, such as Octoprint (I could never get UART to work, it would just cutoff before finishing the preamble regardless of baudrate), TMC2208 UART mode without custom wiring, etc. In the end, I did get a fair amount of use out of this printer and I wanted to make it into something better, thus this printer became the ‘older’ part of this build.

3.0 Design Overview

The following components and subsystems were incorporated into Chimera iXY:

Flsun i3 2017 Mingda Magician X
Stepper motors (E0, Z axis) Stepper motors (α, β axis)
2020 Aluminum extrusions 24V 15A Powersupply
V6 Hotend Bolts: M3-M6
Roller Bearings Leadscrews + Collars
Toothless Idler Pulleys Toothed Idler Pulleys
Stepper Leadscrew Collet Heated Bed
Mainboard MKS Gen L v1.0 30mm axial fan
Reprap Discount Smart Graphics Controller 4010 blower fan
Bolts: M3-M6 625 bearings
Nuts: M3-M6 24V 60W heater cartridge
8mm Linear Rods (Not rails) 8mm Linear Rod Bearings
GT2 6mm Belts (Used Temporarily for measurements and testing) Cables (Stepper, Display, Power, etc.)
PTFE Bowden Tube Bowden Extruder Assembly
PTFE Couplings NTC 100k thermistor

* All fans from the Flsun i3 were not used since I moved from a 12V to a 24V system.
** Refer to BOM for additional required parts.

6.0 Assembly — Electrical

NOTE: This is entirely subjective. You can go with your own board as you want as long as it supports the necessary hardware/firmware.

After installing a defective TMC2208 driver into my MKS Gen L v1.0, I decided to look for a more modern board. The following options were ones I looked at and experimented with physically:

6.1 Mainboard Choices

The choice of mainboard is subjective; boards tried included:

  • MKS Gen L v2.1 — Overall excellent choice and it would’ve been the board I went with. After obtaining one it died shortly after due to unknown reasons (likely defective), thus I had to return it and find another board. There were no more in stock so I decided to look for something more powerful then 8-bit AVR. Having UART mode work with the drivers natively on this board is a plus.
  • MKS TinyBee v1.0 — Upon initial inspection, this board seemed to be the best 32-bit board for this build. Uses an ESP32 and has it’s own web server. I ended up not choosing this as I wasn’t able to get UART mode to work with these drivers. This board doesn’t support it natively and even after soldering wires from each drivers’ UART to the shared TX2/RX2 serial connection I still couldn’t get it to work (Even with a 220 ohm pull up resistor).
  • MKS Robin Nano v3.1 — I ended up going with this board as I got a good deal on Amazon for it with a MKS TFT35 v2.0 display. Supports UART mode natively, plenty of expansion and a web server.

6.2 Drivers

TMC2209s were my preferred option for this. No brand, just generic Aliexpress ones. I initially ordered 2 just for CoreXY kinematics, but I ended up ordering 2 more for the z axis and extruder.

TMC2208s are also a valid option (even standalone), but I plan to implement sensorless homing so the 2209s were best fit for this project.

6.4 Power Supply

Out of the two PSU options, I opted for the 24V PSU from the MMX since the Flsun one was borderline about to explode and frequently ran into thermal overheating issues.

Further testing concluded that it just seems to be a generic powersupply. The cooling fan claims to be ‘automatically’ controlled but it is extremely loud at all times, leading me to believe that it is fixed at full speed. I will either swap this fan for a noctua branded one or cut the cover to fit a 120mm fan.

7.0 Firmware

The repo contains Marlin 2.1.2.5, which is currently used for this printer and is updated in parallel (as soon as changes are final). I might switch to Klipper in later iterations, but issues with the Orange Pi 2 W I wanted to use pushed this back.

A Beaglecam v1 is also used for monitoring the prints. This is obviously not required since the Robin Nano has a built in ESP32 wifi module which I also have to set up, but I had it on hand for now. This camera has many features, including printing over serial and color night vision, so it is a valuable addition to remotely control the 3d printer.

8.0 Known Issues

8.1 OrangePi + MKS Gen L v1.0 OctoPrint Integration

The following is a brief log of issues I encounters while setting up octoprint on an Orange Pi 2 W and MKS Gen L v1.0. It is important to note I was not able to get this working.

  1. Initial USB Serial Connection Fails: OctoPrint hangs at "Connecting"; malformed or partial boot responses observed.
  2. Corrupt Serial Text: Partial, cut-off strings in the serial log.
  3. No Communication Timeout Retry: OctoPrint 1.9+ removed retry behavior used previously; required firmware-level adjustments.
  4. Pronterface Works, OctoPrint Doesn’t: manual terminal connections succeed while OctoPrint fails due to handshake differences.
  5. Port Conflicts: Wyze/Docker integration caused occupied ports (5000, 8888); resolved by removing conflicting services.
  6. OctoPrint Requires Pronterface 'Priming': observed need to prime the printer via terminal before OctoPrint can connect reliably.
  7. Fake Acknowledgement Needed: constant need to send fake ack to proceed; firmware tweaks (#define NO_TIMEOUTS 1000, disable automatic temp reports) used as workarounds.
  8. Serial Buffer Underrun: pauses mid-print; increasing baud rates (to 500000) improved reliability.

8.2 V6 Extrusion Mount Front & Backplate

Despite the plates used to mount the V6 hotend to the extrusion being designed with mm precision, there is still a slight 1mm wobble.

Temporary solution: Add 2 M5 eccentric nuts to the top bolts holding the bearings. After adjustment, this significantly reduces the wobble to around 0.1mm.

There is still a very small wobble that does affect prints. I will be completely redesigning the hotend mount based off these issues, using the extrusion brackets from the Mingda Magician X since they are made of metal and already milled to precision.

8.3 Thermal Runaway

Due to the cheap heater cartridge used, I frequently get into thermal runaways, which is no suprise considering the 3D printer it was from. For some reason it cannot regulate itself and aggressively oscillates at 20C differences. Even after a PID tune and increasing the Derivate constant significantly it continues to do this, usually mid print. This is not safe and will be replaced.

9.0 Conclusion — Chimera iXY Rev.2.0

In conclusion, I was able to get a working rig by the first revision, but there are many issues to be addressed, which also significantly affected the print quality. I am working on v1.1 as we speak, but this is the general timeline that I would like to fix these issues:

  • v1.1: Firmware bugfixes, hotend heater/thermistor replacement, frame rigidity improvements.
  • v1.2: Full extrusion front plate redesign, new high-flow hotend, 100 mm/s quality print speed.
  • v1.3: Bilinear bed leveling, 150 mm/s quality print speed.
  • v1.4: 200 mm/s quality print speed.
